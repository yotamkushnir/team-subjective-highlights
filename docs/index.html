Team Subjective Highlights

Winner-channel vs loser-channel edits (club-blind)

Role-based scope

We compare winner-channel vs loser-channel edits for the same matches, club-blind in the main analysis: teams are only “winner” or “loser” in that fixture, not named brands in the headline claim.

What this study does not try to explain

We did not analyze what happened in the match in terms of type of action (e.g. shot shape, set pieces, “quality” of save or goal). The play-by-play content of the match is given: goals and obviously strong plays will appear. There is no meaningful “editorial voice” in which PBP moments get in at that level. The editorial voice shows up in pacing, how things are split (how many of each kind of beat, not which specific plays), exposure to people (how much time on self vs rival personas), emotional emphasis, and how much room is left to the opponent in the cut.

Cordiality

Any extra exposure to the rival is “generous” in this framework — not only optional chances. Rival reaction time is not the opposite of self celebration; it is another form of screen time you can give the opponent. High rival exposure (reactions, rival celebrations you still show, etc.) reads cordial in the same direction as showing rival non-goal chances — you are ceding space in the edit.

“Rival” always means the other team in this match (your opponent that day), not a historic derby “rival.”

Executive summary

Bottom line first (paired fixtures; exact n varies by column completeness in the sheet — see each figure). % convention: where winner edits are higher on average, we report 100×(W−L)/L (“vs loser baseline”). Where loser edits are higher (e.g. clips, rival replays), we report 100×(high−low)/low using the lower mean as baseline.

    Want to know how a video editor would interpret this data?

    Video editor takeaways

    Plain-language read on winner-channel vs loser-channel cuts — directional only.

    When we win, the reel tends to…

    • Breathe more. Fewer total clips, but each moment stays on screen longer, and the whole video is a bit longer (~4%). It feels less frantic, more “let this moment land.”
    • Lean into joy. After our goals, we spend a lot more time on celebrations (especially in games where both teams scored). The story is: we scored, and we’re feeling it — linger on hugs, runs, crowd, bench.
    • Show our misses a bit less. We include fewer “we almost scored” moments overall — the edit doesn’t need to prove we could have; the result already did.
    • Let the rival sweat (a little). When they fluff a chance, we give more of their frustrated reactions — it reads as “they had their moments but couldn’t take them,” which flatters our win.

    When we lose, the reel tends to…

    • Cut faster and pack more in. More clips, shorter average clip — snappier, more “here’s everything that happened,” less time to sit in any single beat.
    • Spend less time celebrating our goals (again, where both teams scored). We still show we scored, but we don’t milk the happy bits as long — the mood of the day pulls the cut forward.
    • Show more of our near-goals. More own-team non-goal chances — often reads as “we were in it,” “look how close it was,” or simply “we didn’t get the breaks.” It’s the classic “we fought / could’ve been different” layer.
    • Give the opponent a bit more non-goal spotlight. More replays of their non-goal highlights (saves, close calls, etc.) — not necessarily more chances in the data, but more editorial emphasis on those moments. Visually, the opponent’s story gets a bit more air.

    One-line takeaway for the desk

    Wins sell emotion and clarity: longer holds, bigger celebrations, fewer “almost” clips, a touch of rival pain. Losses sell effort and narrative: quicker pace, more “our chances” and “their moments” to frame the game, with celebrations trimmed so the piece doesn’t pretend it was a party.

    Caveat (worth keeping in mind)

    Sample sizes are small (n in the teens–30s for some metrics). Treat this as directional editorial instinct, not a rigid formula — brand, opponent, and how ugly the loss was still override averages.
